New Labour Law Amendments: What Employers Must Know

A practical read on the clauses that most often trip up small employers.

The Employment Act, 2006 is the primary statute governing employer-employee relations in Uganda. Recent amendments and judicial interpretations have shifted the landscape, especially regarding termination of employment, unfair dismissal, and the rights of casual workers. This article highlights key areas every employer should watch.

1. Termination of Employment

Under Section 66 of the Employment Act, termination must be with notice or payment in lieu of notice. The minimum notice period ranges from one day (for casual employees) to three months (for senior staff). Failure to give proper notice can lead to claims for wrongful dismissal.

Termination on grounds of misconduct requires a fair hearing. The employer must provide the employee with an opportunity to defend themselves before any decision is taken. Summary dismissal (without notice) is only permitted for gross misconduct, such as theft, fraud, or serious insubordination.

2. Unfair Dismissal

Section 67 of the Act protects employees from unfair dismissal. A dismissal is unfair if it is not based on the employee’s conduct, capacity, or the operational requirements of the employer. The Labour Disputes (Arbitration and Settlement) Act provides for dispute resolution through the Industrial Court.

Employers are often surprised by the compensation awarded for unfair dismissal – up to 12 months’ salary in some cases. Proper documentation and adherence to disciplinary procedures are the best defence.

3. The Rise of Casual and Contract Workers

Many employers use casual labour without realising that casual employees acquire rights after continuous service of a certain period (e.g., four months under Section 13). The courts have increasingly treated casual workers as permanent employees if they have been engaged on a regular basis for a significant period.

It is essential to have clear written contracts that specify the nature of the engagement and the duration. Failure to do so may lead to claims for severance pay and other benefits.

4. Health and Safety Obligations

The Occupational Safety and Health Act, 2006 imposes a duty on employers to provide a safe working environment. Non-compliance can result in fines and even criminal liability. Employers must conduct risk assessments, provide protective equipment, and train employees on safety procedures.

5. Practical Tips for Employers

  • Draft clear employment contracts that comply with the Act.
  • Maintain accurate records of attendance, leave, and disciplinary actions.
  • Seek legal advice before terminating any employee.
  • Stay updated on amendments and case law.
