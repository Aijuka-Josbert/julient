Family Law

Navigating Family Mediation Before Filing in Court

Why mediation is often faster, cheaper, and kinder than litigation.

Family disputes – whether about divorce, child custody, or maintenance – are emotionally charged. The traditional courtroom can exacerbate conflict, expose private matters to public scrutiny, and leave lasting scars. Mediation offers a constructive alternative.

1. What is Family Mediation?

Mediation is a voluntary process where a trained neutral third party (the mediator) facilitates communication between disputing parties to help them reach a mutually acceptable agreement. In Uganda, the Court-connected Mediation scheme (under the Judicature Act) is available in all High Court divisions and many magistrate’s courts.

Unlike litigation, mediation is confidential, flexible, and can be scheduled at the parties’ convenience. The mediator does not impose a decision – they guide the parties towards their own solution.

2. When is Mediation Most Effective?

  • Divorce and separation – especially where children are involved.
  • Child custody and visitation – parents can design arrangements that work for their unique circumstances.
  • Maintenance and child support – payments can be negotiated to reflect actual needs and abilities.
  • Property division – in cases of separation, mediation can avoid a costly trial.
  • Disputes between extended family – e.g., over inheritance or care of elderly relatives.

3. The Mediation Process

Typically, the parties agree to mediation – either on their own or at the suggestion of a judge or lawyer. The mediator holds joint and separate sessions, exploring interests and identifying common ground. Once an agreement is reached, it is reduced to writing and can be made a consent judgment of the court, giving it legal force.

4. Advantages Over Litigation

  • Faster resolution – weeks instead of years.
  • Lower cost – mediator fees are shared and often fixed.
  • Preservation of relationships – vital where children are involved.
  • Confidentiality – the process is private; no public record.
  • Greater satisfaction – parties are more likely to abide by an agreement they crafted themselves.

5. How to Prepare for Mediation

  • Identify your key interests – what really matters to you?
  • Gather relevant documents – financial records, children’s school reports, etc.
  • Be willing to compromise – mediation is about finding a middle ground.
  • Seek legal advice beforehand – know your rights and options.
